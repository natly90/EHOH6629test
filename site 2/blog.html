Electrical hazards aren't the only thing electricians should be worried about on the job

By Natalie Schwatka

An electrician was recently fatally injured after touching an energized anchor that met an overhead powerline. The worker did not directly touch the power line, yet they were still electrocuted. When you think of an electrician you might think that electricity is the only pressing hazard that they must deal with at work. Indeed, this story paints a vivid picture. However, there are many other safety and ergonomic hazards that electricians face daily.

The construction industry

Each year about 1,000 construction workers are killed on the job. These fatalities are most caused by falls to a lower level or transportation accidents.

In 2015, 985 construction workers died from work-related injuries, accounting for 20% of the total (4,836) fatal injuries at workplaces in the United States (reference).

Construction workers also experience non-fatal injuries on the job — 2.5 per 100 FTEs (full-time equivalents). Some of these injuries are serious enough to require time away from work to recover.

Occupational safety and ergonomics professionals working in the construction industry should be aware of common safety and ergonomic hazards and ways to control exposure to them to reduce employees' risk of injury, illness, or fatality.

Electricians

Fatalities

Compared to all construction occupations, electricians experienced a slightly lower rate of fatal injury.

Non-fatal injuries

Electricians also experienced a slightly lower rate of non-fatal injuries.

What safety hazards are common in electrical work?

Stories of electricians' injuries and fatalities are too common in the construction industry. Electricians are exposed to electrical (Elcosh, 2020a; Elcosh, 2020b; OSHA, 2020a), fall/slip/trip (OSHA, 2015), and noise hazards (OSHA, 2020b; University of Washington, 2004), among others. As you can imagine, electrical hazards seem like the most pressing safety issue during electrical work — they can be found throughout many of their job tasks: exposed wires, wet conditions. If exposed, the risk of severe injury is high — burns and electrocutions can happen.

On the other hand, fall hazards can also be an issue. Electricians often work on ladders and lifts in order to access electrical units and wiring. The combination of electrical hazards and working at heights can be deadly. If an electrician accidentally touches a live wire while on a ladder, not only will they be at risk for a serious burn, but they are also at risk of falling from heights leading to bone fractures or other serious injuries.

What ergonomic hazards are common in electrical work?

Strains and sprains are some of the most reported non-fatal injuries. Electricians can sustain musculoskeletal injuries or develop disorders through a variety of means during their work. They commonly carry heavy boxes around the job site (Construction Solutions, 2020a), they work overhead to install wiring (California Department of Public Health, 2011; Construction Solutions, 2020b), and they kneel/squat while prepping materials and repairing wiring (Construction Solutions, 2020c).

What's regulated by OSHA?

While there are many OSHA regulations to protect workers from the safety hazards that were just discussed, the only OSHA regulation that applies to ergonomic hazards is the general duty clause.

Critical control methods to protect electricians on the job

Contractors commonly turn to personal protective equipment (PPE) to protect electricians from hazards on the job. Whether it's personal fall protection gear, hearing protection, or knee pads — electricians can be seen wearing many of these.

However, PPE is the last line of defense. Electrical contractors should develop policies and programs that will guide safe work. For example, to control electrical hazards, contractors should develop a lock out tag out program to control hazardous energy. They should also consider work policies that guide how to design job flow on a construction site to minimize distances that workers need to carry heavy boxes of material.

The gold standard is to eliminate hazards, but oftentimes you cannot completely eliminate hazards in this industry. So, if the budget allows, it's a good idea to find some engineering controls. For example, engineering controls can be used to reduce exposure to a noisy machine or to reduce strains and sprains by providing upper limb exoskeletons. Or new overhead drills can be purchased to eliminate awkward postures while drilling overhead (NIOSH, 2007).

Last word

While the construction industry is one of the most dangerous industries to work in, we know a lot about how to identify and control exposures to health hazards. While it may seem like a challenge to identify and control all hazards, it will pay off in the form of fewer injuries, increased productivity, and a better quality of product delivered to the customer.

Occupational safety and ergonomics professionals have a unique opportunity to influence electrical workers' health. They should look for electrical, fall, and noise hazards to prevent acute and chronic health conditions. They should look out for the way electricians physically perform their work. Are they carrying heavy items for long distances, are they working overhead, and are they spending time kneeling or squatting? Upon identification — use the full range of the hierarchy of controls to control exposure to these hazards.
