Hazards & Controls

Health hazards for electricians.

Risk of injury, illness, and fatality varies by occupation. Below are the most common safety and ergonomic hazards in the electrical trades — and the controls that reduce exposure.

Safety hazards

Safety Hazard

Electricity

Electrical hazards are found throughout many job tasks — exposed wires, poor insulation, and wet conditions.

Adverse outcomes
Electric shock and burns, arc-flash burns, arc-blast impacts, falls.
OSHA standards / guidelines
29 CFR 1926 Subpart K; Electrical Safety: Safety & Health for Electrical Trades (Student Manual).
Solutions
  • Lockout / tagout program (administrative control)
  • Clothing that does not easily catch fire; safety glasses with side shields (PPE)
  • Use ground-fault circuit interrupters (engineering control)
Safety Hazard

Working at heights

Electricians often work on scaffolds, ladders, and lifts. They also work on job sites where the walking-working surface is cluttered, which presents trip hazards.

Adverse outcomes
Fractures, strains/sprains, death.
OSHA standards / guidelines
29 CFR 1926 Subpart M.
Solutions
  • Personal fall arrest systems, guardrails (PPE)
  • Controlled access zones (administrative control)
  • Electrical safety training (administrative control)
Safety Hazard

Noise

Electricians regularly use powered tools that generate noise above the recommended limit. They are also exposed to noise generated by other construction trades working near them.

Adverse outcome
Hearing loss.
OSHA standards / guidelines
29 CFR 1926.52 & 29 CFR 1926.101; University of Washington's Construction Industry Noise Exposures: Electricians.
Solutions
  • Training on when and how to use hearing protection (administrative control)
  • Reduce noise emanating from machinery: maintenance, purchase quieter machinery (engineering control)
  • Hearing protection (PPE)

Ergonomic hazards

Ergonomic Hazard

Carrying

Electricians carry boxes with wiring from the truck into the job site.

Adverse outcomes
Strain/sprain; musculoskeletal disorder of the low back.
OSHA standards / guidelines
OSHA General Duty Clause, Section 5(a)(1) of the Occupational Safety and Health Act.
Solutions
  • Provide carts (engineering control)
  • Lay out the worksite so that long carrying distances are not needed (engineering control)
  • Participatory ergonomics program to involve workers in identifying solutions (administrative control)
Ergonomic Hazard

Overhead work

Electricians often work overhead, reaching with arms over the shoulders to install electrical wiring — often requiring overhead drills.

Adverse outcomes
Shoulder tendonitis; tension neck syndrome (myofascial pain syndrome).
OSHA standards / guidelines
OSHA General Duty Clause, Section 5(a)(1); California Department of Public Health, Drilling Overhead.
Solutions
  • Purchase an overhead drill press (substitution control)
  • Upper limb exoskeletons (engineering control)
  • Take regular breaks (administrative control)
Ergonomic Hazard

Kneeling & squatting

Electricians regularly kneel and squat while preparing materials and to install and repair electrical wiring.

Adverse outcome
Low back pain; bursitis or osteoarthritis.
OSHA standards / guidelines
OSHA General Duty Clause, Section 5(a)(1) of the Occupational Safety and Health Act.
Solutions
  • Knee pads (PPE)
  • Kneeling creepers (PPE)
  • Limit time in a kneeling position by rotating with another worker (administrative control)
